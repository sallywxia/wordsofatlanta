"But the Second Battle of Atlanta is primarily - and surprisingly - nonviolent. And it is fought with love as well as hate."

-Look Magazine, April, 1961

About Words of Atlanta

"Words of Atlanta” is a data visualization on the Civil Rights Movement in Atlanta, specifically focusing on the year of 1960 and early 1961.

Mainly driven by students and led by Martin Luther King Jr., Atlanta’s Civil Rights Movement is fascinating period of time where people with different beliefs came together to avoid riots in Atlanta.

Realizing the complexity and depth of that specific period of history cannot be illustrated with a simple timeline, the main form of interaction is through quotes and words said and recorded by different groups of people to give a full context of the story as well as to draw light on the significance of the events.

The Student Leader

Lonnie King Jr.

"Lonnie C. King, Jr. (no relation to Martin Luther King) is chairman of the militant Committee on Appeal for Human Rights, which includes some white as well as Negro students.

At 24, this former Morehouse College fullback is sharply aware of his own inadequacies; but he often seems “arrogant” to white Atlantans because he approaches them as an equal rather than an inferior.

Lonnie and the other sophisticated members of the “movement” often surprise adults of their own race, some of whom would feel more comfortable were the movement under their control. But most Negroes and many whites have been stirred by the young Negroes’ courage and spirituality.

Their “Oath to Non-Violence” states, in part: “Non-violence does not seek to defeat or humiliate the opponent, but to win his friend…the attack is directed against forces of evil rather than against the persons who happen to be doing the evil. [It] is based on the conviction that the universe is on the side justice. At [its] center stands the principle of love.”

Benjamin Brown

Benjamin Brown has been in Atlanta’s jail three times. “I consider each time a badge of honor,” he says, “a moral protest against an unconstitutional state anti trespass law designed to support the un-Christian institution of segregation.” At 21, Ben is president of Clark College students and a leader in the Negro student movement. In February, the students staged a mass march to observe the first anniversary of sit-ins in the South. Ben served as picket-team captain.

The White Moderate

Ralph McGill

Publisher of the Atlanta Constitution, enjoys a good fight. As an opponent of segregation, he has had plenty of fights, bushels of menacing (and congratulatory) mail and years of anonymous phone calls. McGill and the Atlanta papers form a strong liberal force in the Second Battle of Atlanta.

William Hartsfield

In many ways, Mayor William B. Hartsfield is, at 71, the youngest man in Atlanta. “Wild Willie” is a nonconformist and master political in the Truman mold, and says he has over “my town” more each of the 23 years he has served it as mayor. His power comes from an unlikely coalition of white business leaders, represented by Chamber of Commerce president Ivan Allen, Jr., right, and the Negro community, symbolized by Atlanta University president Dr. Rufus Clement, left. Behind Hartsfield stands police chief Herbert Jenkins, who models his force after the FBI and disciplines his men to uphold the law “regardless of race or personal feelings.”

The Segregationist

Calvin L. Craig

Calvin L. Craig, who runs Craig Dry Cleaners in Atlanta, has maintained his position as Grand Dragon (top officer) of Georgia Ku Klux Klans through recent organizational shake-ups. Craig is disturbed at the current public image of the Klan.

“We are for the Christian-Protestant religion, the U.S. Government and Constitution thereof and , of course, White Supremacy.” Like Lester Maddox of G.U.T.S., Craig sees communism behind many integration moves. The Klan, he says, opposes violence. (However, eight out of nine men arrested for rioting at the University of Georgia were Klansmen.) Newsmen say that Klan is small but powerful.

Ernest Vandiver

Ernest Vandiver has lived through that painful political experience of eating his own words - and thereby becoming what has been described as a “statesman.” In the campaign of 1958, he promised to use force or close the schools to prevent “any breakdown at any time, at any place in the existing pattern.”

The G.U.T.S. Man

Lester Maddox, head of Georgians Unwilling to Surrender (G.U.T.S.), bids good-by to patrons of his Pickrick Cafeteria with the cheery phrase: “You stay segregated, y’hear?” Maddox sees his fight against integration as “only part of a larger battle” that includes restricting the power of the Supreme Court, removing Federal influence over education in the states, opposing foreign aid and supporting the House Committee on Un-American Activities.

He believes that those who fight for integration do so for political or economic gain. Wherever those motivations don’t apply, he sees a “Communist conspiracy.” “Most people feel as we do,” says Maddox, “but they’re just not organized.” He hopes to form a nationwide organization, is proud of the “respectability” of his 1,000 active members and disclaims any ties with the Klan. (Atlanta police, however, say that over half of those attending G.U.T.S. rallies may also be found at Klan rallies.) Maddox refused to pose before a G.U.T.S. sign, but suggested to Look the wording seen above.
